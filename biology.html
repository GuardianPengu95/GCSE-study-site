Biology – AQA GCSE (Higher)

Animal Cells

Nucleus – contains genetic material (DNA) and controls the activities of the cell.

Mitochondria – site of aerobic respiration, where energy is released.

Ribosomes – site of protein synthesis.

Cell membrane – controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell.

Cytoplasm – gel-like substance where most chemical reactions take place, controlled by enzymes.

Plant Cells

Cell wall – made of cellulose; strengthens the cell and supports its shape.

Chloroplasts – site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll which absorbs light energy.

Permanent vacuole – contains cell sap and maintains turgor pressure to keep the cell rigid.

Plant cells also contain all structures found in animal cells.

Prokaryotic Cells (Bacterial Cells)

Cytoplasm – where chemical reactions take place.

Cell membrane – controls movement of substances into and out of the cell.

Cell wall – supports the cell; not made of cellulose.

Circular DNA – genetic material that controls the cell; not enclosed in a nucleus.

Plasmids – small rings of DNA that can carry genes such as antibiotic resistance.

Ribosomes – smaller than eukaryotic ribosomes; site of protein synthesis.

Prokaryotic cells do not have a nucleus or membrane-bound organelles.

Exam-Grade Questions (Active Recall)

Explain the function of the mitochondria. The mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration, where energy is released for cell activities.
State two structures found in plant cells but not animal cells. Chloroplasts and a permanent vacuole.
Describe one difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells. Prokaryotic cells do not have a nucleus, whereas eukaryotic cells do.
Why are plasmids important in bacteria? They can carry genes such as antibiotic resistance, increasing bacterial survival.
Which cell structure is responsible for protein synthesis? Ribosomes.
